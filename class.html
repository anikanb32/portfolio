Overview

A tool that supports overworked staff who work with individuals recovering from traumatic brain injuries

For our HCI capstone project, we partnered with CLASS, a nonprofit in Pittsburgh, PA, that runs a Structured Day Program for individuals with TBI. Staff faced major challenges: fragmented documentation, heavy paperwork, and a lack of technology to streamline participant support.

We designed a solution that would reduce staff burden by consolidating participant information, empower participants to engage with their goals and activities, and ensure accessibility for a wide range of cognitive abilities.

Context

Disabilities affect more than 1.3 billion people worldwide.

CLASS is a nonprofit organization that helps individuals with disabilities and older adults live independently in their communities and achieve their goals. Community plays a vital role in the rehabilitation and growth process for individuals with cognitive impairments and motor disabilities, which is exactly what CLASS facilitates.

The goal of our project, given to us by our client, was to research and develop a potential solution that would help any program at CLASS. We were given a very broad scope with seemingly unlimited directions to take. So our first step was to narrow our focus area.

CLASS offers a wide variety of programs for participants, including in-home services, community living support, and training programs among many others. We focused on the Structured Day program a newer specialized program that supports individuals with Traumatic Brain Injuries.

We began with extensive research to understand the daily challenges faced by CLASS staff and the unique needs of TBI participants.

So how do we contextualize these pain points and research insights and how can we connect the needs of the participants with the responsibilities of staff members?

Problem

Participants highly value personalized support from staff, but staff are not always in the position to give it.

No centralized system

Staff relied on memory or scattered Word docs.

Inefficient assessments

Over 20 pages to document each participant, subjective and hard to track.

Limited participant autonomy

Participants had little control in shaping their daily activities.

Ideation

When thinking about what a prototype would look like, we first considered some questions the prototype should be able to answer.

1.

How can a prototype increase or improve the quality of time spent between participants and staff?

2.

How can staff and participants currently track the participants' personal goals, and what challenges do they face?

3.

What data visualization or information organization would be most beneficial for both participants and staff?

What would our prototype look like based on our probing questions?

Prototyping

How do we determine what information to include while prototyping?

To bridge the gap between our research insights and prototyping, we decided to conduct a co-design activity using a card-sorting exercise.

We conducted the activity with 4 TBI participants and 2 CLASS staff members to tackle a key challenge: with so much diverse program information circulating, we needed to identify what mattered most to each group. We created two card sets, one for staff and another for participants, with a total of 34 cards.

Big-picture takeaways that helped shape our first set of prototypes.

Staff benefit from program-level information about activities.

Participants want to represent themselves and their goals.

Staff want to be able to plan better based on attendance.

Usability Testing

What features do staff and participants value?

We conducted usability testing with our lo-fi prototypes to understand which features resonated most with both user groups.

Staff Prototype Insights

Would need to know what materials and activities they are doing beforehand

Staff appreciate having activity suggestions

Roll call style attendance can be unreliable because participants come at different times

Participant Prototype Insights

Participants want a space to share their achievements and projects

Participants would prefer large font and color customization

Designs

A new and improved interaction method

Profiles

insight

Participant logs for staff are helpful to reference periodically to help with handling activities or personal and social interactions.

A detailed participant profile to document high level information about skills, goals, and interests.

Activity Suggestion

insight

Staff need a way to track past activities and get goal-based suggestions.

Display of suggested activities by different filters based on participant information helps prioritize activity types.

Viewing Attendance

insight

Staff oversight on attendance is important. Staff should be able to view crucial information about participants.

Implemented an attendance tracking system along with a detailed participant profile.

Activity Information

insight

It is important to keep in mind the level of support to determine activities based on feasibility.

Staff need to know when an activity was last conducted to ensure variety in programming.

Prototype Outcomes

Suggesting relevant and interesting activities for each participant can increase and improve the quality of time spent between participants and staff.

Staff and participants can track the participants' personal goals on their individual profiles, which show their skills, progress, and interests.

Participants and staff can use the dashboard to organize and reference information about participants, plan activities ahead of time, and help assimilate new staff easily.

Final Thoughts

Learnings...

Having spoken to the participants with traumatic brain injuries and hearing their own individual goals, I realized how important it is to give people autonomy through design, especially when their voices are not always heard. This project shifted my perspective on who the 'user' really is in complex systems and sometimes the most impactful design work happens behind the scenes, empowering the people who directly support those who need it most.

With more time...

I would want to run more user testing with the most refined coded prototype specifically to test metrics on staff time spent planning and researching daily activities. I would love to help with the implementation of the backend, especially because the assistive technology coordinator we were working with, Rebekah, emphasized how difficult it is to hand off to an external software specialist. Creating a more robust prototype would allow for staff to use the prototype more readily while the final deliverable is completed.
